Links and Resources

Science often gets a bad rap for being communicated poorly to others, whether that be the general public, or other scientists and learners. I've aggregated a collection of links and resources I've used in my work to help me communicate my story. This is as much an online reference list for me as it is a collection of resources others may find useful as well.  

A note: I am in no way affiliated with any of these sites in any way, but I do recommend them, the rationale for which is included below. 

Academic

  • Anki - Anki has completely changed the way I study by making it extremely efficient and easy. The gist of it is that you make flashcards based upon course content (e.g. lecture slides, textbook, etc,), and using their algorithm based on spaced repitition, Anki shows you content when you're likely to forget it. Shamim Ahmed wrote a great post about it here, but to really get a strong fouindation in its use, I recommend Learning Medicine: An Evidence-Based Guide. The eBook is the best $15 you can spend to equip yourself for success in life science classes. Note that there are other apps out there using the Anki name. Don't use them; the learning curve for this version is well worth the time investment. 
  • The Pomodoro Technique - sitting and focusing for long periods of time is tough. Taking scheduled breaks helps make it better, and I have found the Pomodoro technique stellar when making flashcards for lots of lecture content. I use Tomato One for macOS, but TomatoTimer is just as useful. Make sure to turn on notifications in your browser!
  • Mendeley - I recall high school where we would use EasyBib to generate citations for our work. It was fine then, but university demands a more powerful tool. Mendeley allows you to import citations from papers and books (via Google Books) to their app, and to automatically generate and insert the citations into Microsoft Word. It too takes some time investment, but I found it to be well worth thr time I spent learning it. You may have to manually clean up citations in APA. 
  • Trello - A suprising amount of acadamia is project management. I like Trello for its featureful free plan, which allows you to visually seperate and organize projects to ensure you are on top of all aspects of it. Collaboration is also free in Trello, making it particularly useful for team use. 

Creative

  • PowerPoint Templates from Microsoft - So many people don't know this, but Microsoft themselves publshes hundreds of templates for all types of presentations. 
  • Poster Presentation Templates for PowerPoint - When I had to make my first poster presentation, I asked an upper year student where to start. They led me to PowerPoint, but I'm upset I didn't find this site sooner. Super easy-to-use templates to make sharp looking posters. Make sure to double check the size requirements for the conference you're presenting at and download the correctly-sized template. 
  • Library of Science & Medical Illustrations - This gallary of illustrations spans the health and life sciences, and can be used in just about every field in those disciplines. They can be "bought" for zero dollars: just enter a "0" at the checkout screen, but if you are able, the designers would appreciate anything you would be able to pay for them. 
  • Pixabay - Using clipart is so 2008. These stock images are free to use and look great on presentations, websites and posters. All the images from this site were obtained through Pixabay.
